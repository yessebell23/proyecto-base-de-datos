                              Terry Relph-Knight Trueteller 9 months ago

                              You MAY have the pickup / pole pieces too close to the strings. Too strong a magnetic field from the pickup can damp the strings and cause odd tones. This may be happening high on the neck because that is where you push the strings close to the pickups. If this is the problem then it is likely to be the neck pickup. People often raise the neck pickup too high in order to get massive output. Easy to check - just back off the pickup height adjusting screws by a couple of turns and see if the problem goes.

                                  David P Makowski a year ago

                                  I am the original owner of the Heritage Cherry Sunburst finished Les Paul Standard. I own and have played a 1974 Les Paul Custom since high school (class of 1985). I always wanted a Standard and I am so glad I waited. This 2013 model is super versatile with the (4) push/pull potentiometers. When I played one at Sam Ash Music, I just knew this was the Les Paul for me. I absolutely love alnico V magnet pickups. They're my favorites. Well, both the Burstbucker #1 and Burstbucker #2 have alnico V magnets. Not usually a fan of split coil humbuckers, but Gibson did such a great job ensuring the volume doesn't drop out when in single coil mode. The neck pickup tone control for "phase reversal" is really special. I don't even know exactly what "phase reversal" is however what I do know is it provides a very usable tone. The bridge pickup tone control when pulled up sends the signal straight out the input jack bypassing the tone capacitors and the entire circuit for a blistering raw lead tone. All I have done is keep the guitar polished, added pointers to the volume and tone knobs so I can accurately adjust my volume and tone without having to guess, and switched the Grover "Kidney" buttons to genuine Grover "Keystone" buttons as a personal aesthetic appearance. I love this guitar!!
